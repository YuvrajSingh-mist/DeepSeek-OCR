## Assessment of the uncertainty  

The results of the analyses of the uncertainty of the field data and related assumptions are shown in Figs 13 and 14.  

Polygons \(\eta_{\mathrm{P}}\) , \(\eta_{\mathrm{M}}\) , and \(\eta_{\mathrm{D}}\) in the \(x - y\) plane represent the spatial distribution of any possible \(x_{\mathrm{adv}}\) , that was obtained from the data sets measured at sites P, M, and D, respectively. The degree of overlap between polygons is expressed as \(\rho_{\mathrm{PM}}\) (for overlap between \(\eta_{\mathrm{P}}\) and \(\eta_{\mathrm{M}}\) ) and \(\rho_{\mathrm{MD}}\) (for overlap between \(\eta_{\mathrm{M}}\) and \(\eta_{\mathrm{D}}\) ) (see Eq. (11)).  

There is no overlap between \(\eta_{\mathrm{P}}\) and \(\eta_{\mathrm{M}}\) (i.e. \(\rho_{\mathrm{PM}} = 0\) ) when \(y_{1} \leq 0.35 \mathrm{km}\) and \(y_{1} \geq 1.05 \mathrm{km}\) . With \(y_{1} = 0.40 - 1.00 \mathrm{km}\) , on the other hand, \(\eta_{\mathrm{P}}\) and \(\eta_{\mathrm{M}}\) overlap each other (i.e. \(0 < \rho_{\mathrm{PM}} < 1\) ). However, the highest value of \(\rho_{\mathrm{PM}}\) (at \(y_{1} = 0.40 \mathrm{km}\) ) is as low as 0.137. Excluding the cases that \(y_{1} = 0.40 - 0.45 \mathrm{km}\) , the overlap is negligible (\(\rho_{\mathrm{PM}} \approx 0\) ). On the other hand, the overlap of \(\eta_{\mathrm{M}}\) and \(\eta_{\mathrm{D}}\) is in a significantly different trend from that between \(\eta_{\mathrm{P}}\) and \(\eta_{\mathrm{M}}\) . There is some overlap between \(\eta_{\mathrm{M}}\) and \(\eta_{\mathrm{D}}\) ( \(0 < \rho_{\mathrm{MD}}\) ) when \(y_{1} = 0.20 - 0.90 \mathrm{km}\) . At any magnitude of \(y_{1}\) within this range, \(\rho_{\mathrm{MD}}\) is much larger than that of \(\rho_{\mathrm{PM}}\) (Fig. 14). The highest values of \(\rho_{\mathrm{MD}}\) (0.55- 0.60) are seen when \(y_{1} = 0.50 - 0.70 \mathrm{km}\) . With \(y_{1} = 0.45 \pm 0.10 \mathrm{km}\) , \(\rho_{\mathrm{MD}}\) ranges between 0.31 and 0.55, still much larger than the highest of \(\rho_{\mathrm{PM}}\) .
